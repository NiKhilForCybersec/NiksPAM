PCI-DSS (Payment Card Industry)

PCI-DSS governs how organizations handle credit card data. PAM and KMS address multiple requirements:

PAM Requirements Mapping

Requirement Control How PAM Satisfies
2.1 Change vendor-supplied defaults PAM discovers default accounts, forces password changes, rotates credentials
7.1 Limit access to system components Role-based access control, policy-based authorization, least privilege enforcement
7.2 Access control system for cardholder data PAM vault restricts who can access credentials for systems with cardholder data
8.2 Unique identification for all users Individual PAM accounts, no shared privileged credentials
8.3 Multi-factor authentication MFA required for PAM access, especially for remote and administrative access
8.5 No shared accounts PAM provides individual accountability even when using shared privileged accounts
10.1 Audit trails for system components Complete audit log of all privileged access, who did what, when
10.2.2 Log actions by individuals with root/admin Session recording captures all administrative actions in detail

KMS Requirements Mapping

Requirement Control How KMS Satisfies
3.4 Render PAN unreadable KMS-managed encryption for cardholder data at rest
3.5 Protect cryptographic keys HSM storage, separation of duties, key access logging
3.6 Key management procedures Documented key lifecycle: generation, storage, rotation, destruction
3.6.4 Cryptographic key changes Automated key rotation per policy (annual minimum)
3.6.5 Key retirement/replacement Key versioning, orderly destruction with crypto-period management
3.6.6 Split knowledge and dual control M-of-N key custodian schemes, multi-party authorization for sensitive operations
3.6.7 Prevention of unauthorized substitution HSM prevents key extraction, cryptographic binding, integrity verification

SOX (Sarbanes-Oxley)

SOX requires controls over financial reporting systems. PAM demonstrates who can access and modify financial data:

Section 404 - Internal Controls

Control Objective PAM/KMS Capability Evidence
Access to financial applications is restricted PAM policies limit who can access ERP, financial database credentials PAM access policies, user-to-safe mappings
Privileged access is controlled All admin access through PAM with approval workflows Approval records, access requests with justification
Changes are authorized and logged Session recording shows exactly what changes were made Session recordings, command logs
Segregation of duties Role separation—different users for request vs. approve Role definitions, dual control configurations
Periodic access reviews PAM provides access reports for certification Access review reports, certification evidence
Data integrity protection KMS encrypts financial data, access logged Encryption configurations, key usage logs

SOX Auditor Focus

SOX auditors focus on access control (who can make changes?), change management (were changes authorized?), and audit trail (can you prove what happened?). PAM directly addresses all three. Be prepared to show: access policies, approval workflows, and sample session recordings.

HIPAA (Healthcare)

HIPAA protects Protected Health Information (PHI). Technical safeguards require access controls and encryption:

Technical Safeguards (45 CFR § 164.312)

Safeguard Requirement PAM/KMS Solution
Access Control (a)(1) Implement technical policies for access to ePHI PAM role-based access, policy engine, approval workflows
Unique User ID (a)(2)(i) Assign unique identifier for each user Individual PAM accounts, no shared credentials
Emergency Access (a)(2)(ii) Procedures for obtaining ePHI during emergency Break-glass accounts with full audit trail
Automatic Logoff (a)(2)(iii) Terminate sessions after inactivity PAM idle timeout, session time limits
Encryption (a)(2)(iv) Encrypt ePHI KMS-managed encryption for ePHI at rest and in transit
Audit Controls (b) Record and examine activity in systems with ePHI Complete PAM audit logs, session recordings
Integrity (c)(1) Protect ePHI from improper alteration KMS provides integrity verification, change logging
Person/Entity Auth (d) Verify identity of persons seeking access Strong authentication, MFA for privileged access
Transmission Security (e)(1) Guard against unauthorized access during transmission KMS-managed TLS certificates, encrypted sessions

Administrative Safeguards Supported

  • Workforce security: PAM provides termination procedures (revoke access, rotate credentials)
  • Information access management: PAM enforces access authorization policies
  • Security incident procedures: PAM logs provide incident investigation evidence
  • Contingency plan: Break-glass procedures ensure access during emergencies

GDPR (Data Privacy)

GDPR focuses on personal data protection. PAM and KMS support key requirements:

Article Requirement PAM/KMS Support
Article 5(1)(f) Integrity and confidentiality KMS encryption protects personal data; PAM controls access
Article 25 Data protection by design Encryption by default, access controls built in
Article 30 Records of processing activities PAM logs document who accessed personal data
Article 32 Security of processing Encryption (KMS), access control (PAM), regular testing
Article 33 Breach notification PAM logs identify scope of breach (who accessed what)
Article 34 Communication to data subject If encrypted with KMS, risk to subjects may be lower

GDPR and Encryption

GDPR recital 83 specifically mentions encryption as a way to render data unintelligible to unauthorized persons. If you have a breach but data was encrypted with keys the attacker couldn't access, the risk to individuals is reduced, which may affect notification requirements.

NERC CIP (Energy Sector)

Critical Infrastructure Protection standards for the energy sector have specific requirements for privileged access:

Key CIP Standards

Standard Requirement PAM/KMS Solution
CIP-004-6 R4 Access management program PAM provides complete access management for BES Cyber Systems
CIP-004-6 R5 Access revocation PAM enables immediate access revocation, credential rotation
CIP-005-6 R1 Electronic Security Perimeter PAM provides controlled access through ESP
CIP-005-6 R2 Remote access management PAM intermediary, MFA, session monitoring for remote access
CIP-007-6 R5 System access controls PAM enforces authentication requirements, password policies
CIP-011-2 R1 Information protection KMS encryption for BES Cyber System Information

OT-Specific Considerations

  • Air-gapped deployments: PAM can operate in isolated networks
  • Legacy protocol support: PAM proxies can handle Telnet, VNC, proprietary protocols
  • Vendor remote access: JIT access for vendor maintenance with full recording
  • Safety system isolation: Separate PAM policies for safety-critical systems

Audit Evidence Collection

When auditors ask for evidence, here's what to provide from PAM and KMS:

Common Auditor Requests

Auditor Asks Evidence to Provide Source
"Show me who has admin access" List of users with privileged access by system/role PAM access report
"How do you control privileged access?" PAM policies showing approval workflows, MFA, time limits PAM policy export
"Show me access to financial systems in Q4" Filtered access log showing all access to specific systems PAM audit log report
"How do you rotate passwords?" Rotation policy configuration, rotation history PAM rotation reports
"Show me a privileged session" Session recording playback PAM recording vault
"How are encryption keys managed?" Key lifecycle policy, HSM configuration, rotation history KMS policy docs, audit logs
"Who approved this access?" Approval workflow record with approver, timestamp, justification PAM workflow history
"Show me access reviews" Periodic certification reports, remediation evidence PAM certification reports

Evidence Retention

Framework Typical Retention What to Retain
PCI-DSS 1 year (3 months immediately available) Audit logs, access logs
SOX 7 years All financial system access evidence
HIPAA 6 years Access logs, security incident records
GDPR As long as processing continues + defense period Processing records, security measures evidence
NERC CIP 3 years Access logs, change records

Key Takeaways

PAM addresses access control, accountability, and audit requirements across virtually every compliance framework. KMS addresses encryption and key management requirements. Together, they cover major portions of PCI-DSS, SOX, HIPAA, NIST, ISO 27001, GDPR, and NERC CIP. Design your PAM/KMS deployment with compliance in mind, and evidence collection becomes straightforward.
